The Problem

As autonomous vehicles advance toward widespread deployment, they must be programmed to handle unavoidable accident scenarios. Should these life-or-death decisions be governed by simple demographic-blind rules or complex context-sensitive algorithms?

Two Competing Approaches:

Rigid Demographic-Blind Rules

German Ethics Commission (2017): "Treat all humans equally, minimize casualties, avoid discrimination"

Context-Sensitive Algorithms

Utilitarian approach: Consider age, occupation, health, legal responsibility, pregnancy, network effects

This research examines which approach is more ethically defensible through systematic scenario analysis

Conclusions & Next Steps

Key Conclusions:

  • Informational connectedness is a critical policy variable that fundamentally shapes AV ethical behavior (50% of scenarios changed)
  • Utilitarian quantification creates value hierarchies (doctor 1.5x, criminal 0.94x, pregnancy 1.8x) that may conflict with equal human dignity
  • No clear threshold exists where increased connectedness consistently improves ethical outcomes‚Äîit both resolves ambiguity AND introduces controversial results
  • The choice is not technical but political: Society must explicitly decide which factors AVs should consider

Next Steps for Research:

Research suggests the German Ethics Commission's demographic-blind approach may be more ethically defensible‚Äîbut the question requires ongoing societal deliberation, not just algorithmic optimization.
